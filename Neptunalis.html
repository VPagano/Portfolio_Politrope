Neptunalis:
A Venice's lagoon Atlas

A temporal remoteness re-cartography of Venice's lagoon morphological development

Maps have always been problematic for cartographic research because they rarely allow for ambivalence or contradictions, despite their interpretative nature. Maps have promoted a universal and immutable version of the earth’s surface, encouraging a perceptual detachment of each environment. Critical cartography and counter-mapping theories have been a meaningful effort to question the dominant cartographic procedures, making domination visible and providing alternative realities of the territories. However, they still visualize the landscapes as a knowable world, repeating established mapping and cartographic orthodoxies.
Therefore, How can communication design encourage alternative cartographic procedures, telling ecological narratives?

THE PROJECT

Neptunalis: A Venice’s lagoon Atlas is a prototype of a software platform for a temporal remoteness narrative of Venice’s lagoon morphological development. The software, built on a web-based technology, is a web application that allows ecological illiterate users to acquire general knowledge about the morphological history of the Venice lagoon, by explore the evolution of two geographic components that have undergone considerable changes in recent decades. However, the atlas is also structured for the user to move freely within the platform, thanks to the home page, which allows the user to choose sections to browse and explore in depth.

THE RE-CARTOGRAPHY

Venice’s lagoon map is a spatio-temporal re-cartography of Venice’s lagoon morphological development from 1811 to 2003. The re-cartography was created by attempting to tell the story of the lagoon from a horizontal perspective. The various fragments of each of the six historical maps were placed reproducing a horizontal section of the Venetian lagoon, based on qualitative analysis of the colour of each map fragment.
